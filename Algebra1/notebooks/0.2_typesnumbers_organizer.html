Teach Sheet (definitions, examples, non‑examples)
Set Definition (tight & teachable) Examples Non‑Examples (why?) Notes / Emphasis
ℕ Natural Counting numbers starting at 1. 1, 2, 3, 17 0 (not counting), −2 (not positive) Use for quantities you can count: people, books.
W Whole Natural numbers plus 0. 0, 1, 2, 45 −1 (not whole), 1/2 (not whole) Zero makes operations complete (additive identity).
ℤ Integers Whole numbers and their negatives. … −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3 … 1/3 (not whole), 2.4 (not integer) Opposites live here; absolute value is distance.
ℚ Rational Any number that can be written as a fraction of integers (denominator ≠ 0). Includes all terminating and repeating decimals. 3/4, −2/3, 1.5, −0.125, 0.333… √2, π (cannot be made into exact fraction) “Ratio‑nal” → ratio of integers. Decimal pattern: ends or repeats.
Irrational On the number line, but not expressible as a ratio of integers; decimals neither terminate nor repeat. √2, √3, π, φ 0.101010… (repeats → rational) Show why √2 is irrational: diagonal of a 1×1 square.
ℝ Real All rationals and irrationals together; every point on the number line. Everything above i (imaginary), 2+3i (complex) We live in ℝ this year; complex comes later.
Discussion & Emphasis
  • Fraction test: If you can write it as a/b with integers (b≠0), it’s rational. If not (but it’s on the line), it’s irrational.
  • Decimals: terminate or repeat → rational. Neither → irrational.
  • Geometry hook: √2 from a 1×1 square diagonal; π from any circle (ratio circumference/diameter).
  • Set nesting: Know the containment chain and use it to reason about membership.
  • Language: natural vs whole, integer vs rational — be precise.
