üì¶ Module 0 ‚Äì Build: Patterns, Precision, and Reasoning

üéØ Overview

Module 0 establishes the foundations for the year during Weeks 1‚Äì5. Students build routines, tool skills, and diagram literacy, while developing precise vocabulary and habits for definition writing. The weeks focus on routines, vocabulary, angle measurement, and classic compass and straightedge constructions (including applications). Students connect midpoint using coordinate methods. The module culminates in IM 1.9, a real-world application challenge (celebration-style culminating task, light scoring emphasis), during Family Weekend, with group SP awards and celebration (shortened week with visitors; pacing adjusted for light, collaborative work). Emphasis is on building community, fluency with both physical and digital geometry tools, and micro-skills needed for later proof and transformation work. Week 4 includes intentional time padding to absorb squeeze (assemblies, retakes, slowed constructions): quick wins to shift/trim include Flashcard Factory minutes, DeltaMath work time, or pacing the dictionary build across two days. Additionally, angle types (vertical, linear pair, complementary, supplementary) and triangle categories (scalene, isosceles, equilateral) are formally defined in Week 3, with special quadrilaterals added in Week 4 before Family Weekend.

  • Weeks 1‚Äì2: Routines, folder/notebook setup, seating, Skill Gates, DeltaMath intro, SP routines, discovery-based diagram exploration, formal vocabulary (point, line, plane, ray, segment, endpoints, coplanar, collinear, intersection, congruent, parallel, perpendicular, bisector, circle, center, radius), Flashcard Factory sessions.
  • Weeks 2‚Äì3: Diagram labeling, vocabulary expansion, Make Your Own / Match Mine instruction writing, ‚ÄúWhat makes a good definition?‚Äù activities, collaborative vocabulary building.
  • Weeks 3‚Äì4: Angle measurement and naming, classic compass/straightedge constructions (equilateral triangle, perpendicular bisector, angle bisector), diagram micro-skills. Week 4 functions as a buffer week‚Äîdictionary finishing, short DM work blocks, or a compact FF round can flex to protect core instruction. Angle types and triangle categories are defined in Week 3.
  • Week 4‚Äì5: Coordinate midpoint connection, IM 1.6 construction stations, special quadrilateral definitions and hierarchy organizer, consolidation of all skills, IM 1.9 performance challenge, Family Weekend, and group SP awards.

üìö Skills & Topics

  • Classroom, notebook, and SP system routines: daily tape-in structure, module dividers, dictionary half-sheets, weekly SP checks, and group role rotation.
  • Diagram literacy: interpreting, labeling, and marking geometric diagrams; naming conventions for points, lines, rays, segments, angles, triangles, and polygons; reading standard diagram marks (tick marks for congruent segments, arrows for ‚à•, right‚Äëangle squares for ‚üÇ, midpoint hash marks when provided).
  • Vocabulary development: precise definitions for core geometry terms (point, line, plane, ray, segment, endpoints, coplanar, collinear, intersection, congruent, parallel, perpendicular, bisector, circle, center, radius), shape vocabulary (polygon, triangle types, quadrilaterals, vertex, side, interior/exterior, special quadrilaterals (trapezoid, kite, parallelogram, rectangle, rhombus, square)), and meta-vocabulary (example/non-example, counterexample, classify/differentiate, necessary/sufficient, legal/illegal move, equidistant, constraint, assumption, precision).
  • Definition writing: using classify/differentiate with examples and non-examples, collaborative drafting, peer testing, and refining for necessary and sufficient conditions; writing clear dictionary entries for vocabulary and polygons.
  • Instruction writing: sequencing legal diagram moves, writing and following precise instructions (Make Your Own / Match Mine), and reconstructing diagrams from written steps.
  • Angle measurement: using protractors (physical and digital), correct naming (vertex placement, m‚à† notation), baseline alignment, inside/outside scale, accuracy within ¬±2¬∞, and estimation skills.
  • Compass and straightedge constructions: constructing circles, equilateral triangles, perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, regular hexagons, copying angles, constructing parallels and perpendiculars through a point, and emphasizing labeled construction lines and tool handling.
  • Applications of constructions: solving design and mapping problems (e.g., tiling, motifs, finding loci), and connecting perpendicular bisectors to equidistant points in real-world and cultural contexts.
  • Using both physical and digital tools: compass, straightedge, pencils, DeltaMath interactive construction modules, and optional GeoGebra introduction.
  • Coordinate midpoint connection: using the midpoint formula, segment partitioning, and linking midpoint to perpendicular bisector and equidistant concepts; integrating coordinate calculations into geometric reasoning.
  • Readiness micro-skills: naming conventions, diagram interpretation, vocabulary use in justifications, coordinate midpoint calculation, and diagram-based reasoning; readiness checks via DeltaMath Skill Gates.
  • SP system and group recognition: peer coaching (green pen), visible revisions (color-swap), organized tape-ins, SP highlights, table awards, and equity through rotating roles.

Compass/straightedge fluency

  • Philosophy: Constructions are a thinking gym, not a perfection test. The goal is exposure, geometric intuition, and recognizing when/why a construction solves a problem. Precision is encouraged but not required for mastery credit.
  • Structure: Each construction is completed twice‚Äîonce physically (compass/straightedge) and once digitally (DeltaMath) for a friction‚Äëfree guided run‚Äëthrough.
  • Coverage: Draw circle (fixed center/radius), straightedge through two points, transfer radius, perpendicular bisector, angle bisector, equilateral triangle, regular hexagon, parallel through a point, perpendicular through a point, and copy an angle.
  • Tracks: Core = circle, perpendicular bisector, angle bisector, equilateral/hexagon; Stretch = parallel/perpendicular through a point, copy an angle (graded for reasoning, not perfection).
  • Applications: Revisited in collaborative challenges where groups apply constructions in context. Reasoning & application take precedence over flawless execution.
  • Classroom culture: Productive struggle is welcomed. Students compare approaches and explain purposes/logic; success ‚â† perfect compass control.
  • SP & Assessment: SP for persistence, clear labeling, and reasoning. Skill Gates check understanding of purposes and diagram reading rather than demanding perfect physical replication.

  • Coordinate geometry connections: calculating slope, determining conditions for parallelism and perpendicularity, and justifying geometric relationships using coordinate methods.
  • Introduction to formal proof writing: organizing logic using angle relationships, transformation sequences, and algebraic steps; using diagrams and clear vocabulary in written arguments.

üì¶ Module 2 ‚Äì Prove: Congruence, Connections, and Argument

üéØ Overview

Beginning in Week 14 and running through Week 16, Module 2 focuses on triangle congruence proofs, congruence criteria, and the communication of mathematical arguments using multiple proof formats. Students develop fluency with SSS, SAS, ASA, and AAS (HL as time allows), mark and name diagram correspondences, and synthesize their learning before semester end. The module emphasizes careful diagram reading, proof mapping, and synthesis tasks.

  • Lessons: diagram reading, marking correspondence, proof vocabulary, scaffolded proof formats (flowchart, paragraph, annotated diagram).
  • Anchor activities and Skill Gates to support proof fluency; congruence card sorts, error analysis, and synthesis tasks.
  • Buffer days for remediation, extra practice, and check-ins before break.

üìö Skills & Topics

  • Triangle congruence criteria: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS (HL included as time allows), including use of precise notation and vocabulary.
  • Marking, naming, and maintaining correspondence in diagrams and written arguments; using correct order for congruence statements.
  • Mastery of multiple proof formats: flowchart proofs, paragraph proofs, and annotated-diagram proofs, with clear logical sequencing and justification of each step.
  • Use of auxiliary constructions: adding lines, points, or segments to facilitate proofs and connect geometric reasoning to coordinate geometry.
  • Diagram reading and precision: identifying given information, marking congruent parts, and interpreting symbolic and visual cues.
  • Communicating mathematical arguments: using vocabulary, logical structure, and clear diagram references to explain and justify each proof step.

üì¶ Module 3 ‚Äì Similarity in the Wild

üéØ Overview

Module 3 focuses squarely on similarity and proportional reasoning (Weeks 17‚Äì19). Students formalize dilations, develop triangle similarity criteria (AA, SSS~, SAS~), and use parallel-line proportionality to solve for unknowns in geometric and real contexts. Indirect Measurement (mirror method) serves as the applied capstone. Transformation work appears only as quick warm‚Äëup reviews (e.g., Desmos Transformations Golf holes) since full transformations were mastered in Module 1.

  • Weeks 17‚Äì18: Dilations and similarity; triangle similarity criteria; proportional relationships among corresponding parts; parallel-line proportionality in triangles.
  • Week 19: Applications and problem solving; Indirect Measurement Poster Project (mirror method); targeted review for trig launch.

üìö Skills & Topics

  • Conceptual and procedural understanding of dilations: center, scale factor, and mapping figures under dilation.
  • Triangle similarity criteria: AA, SSS~, SAS~; identifying and justifying similarity using side and angle relationships.
  • Proportional reasoning: setting up and solving proportions using side ratios in similar triangles and figures.
  • Parallel-line proportionality: applying proportionality theorems within triangles and polygons with parallel segments.
  • Indirect measurement: applying similarity and proportional reasoning to solve real-world problems (e.g., mirror method), including measurement, diagram modeling, and solution explanation.
  • Coordinate connections: using coordinate geometry to support similarity arguments and proportional calculations.

üì¶ Module 4: Trigonometry in Action

üéØ Overview

Students ground sine, cosine, and tangent in similar right triangles, then apply trig to solve missing sides/angles and model real contexts. The Miracle on the Hudson task and the Trig in Action poster project serve as central capstones. Pacing includes review days and buffers for absences. Presentations are optional for SP; top posters displayed.

  • Trig introduced after similarity; review days built in.
  • Miracle on the Hudson (mid-module); poster project at end.
  • SP routines and DeltaMath review for trig vocabulary and problems.
  • Calculator fluency: degree/radian modes and inverse trig are embedded in practice.

üìö Skills & Topics

  • Right triangle similarity: identifying similar right triangles and using ratio tables to compare side lengths.
  • Trigonometric ratios: defining and applying sine, cosine, and tangent (SOH-CAH-TOA) to find missing side lengths and angles.
  • Inverse trigonometric functions: using inverse sine, cosine, and tangent to determine angle measures from side ratios.
  • Angles of elevation and depression: interpreting and solving real-world modeling problems involving these concepts.
  • Special right triangles: recognizing and solving problems involving 45-45-90 and 30-60-90 triangles, including side ratio shortcuts.
  • Application modeling: setting up, solving, and explaining real-world problems using trigonometric reasoning and geometric diagrams.

üì¶ Module 5: Circles in Motion

üéØ Overview

Circles become a conceptual and symbolic capstone, synthesizing constructions, proof habits, similarity, and trigonometry. Students investigate chords, arcs, central/inscribed angles, tangent lines, and radian measure. Connections to right triangles and similarity are explicit; understanding is demonstrated through problem solving and the culminating Circle Design Challenge. Include a short ‚ÄòFigure Out My Measurement‚Äô day (tangents) to surface right‚Äëtriangle structures.

  • Circle vocabulary/theorems early; scaffold chord/tangent investigations.
  • Labs on radians, arc/sector calculations with manipulatives/technology.
  • Circle Design Challenge: final project; allow multiple sessions for design/presentation.
  • SP and DeltaMath review cycles reinforce key concepts.

üìö Skills & Topics

  • Circle vocabulary: radius, diameter, chord, secant, tangent, arc, sector, central angle, inscribed angle, cyclic quadrilateral, and related terms.
  • Understanding and applying circle theorems: relationships between central and inscribed angles, arc measures, and properties of chords, tangents, and secants.
  • Tangent and chord properties: identifying and proving properties involving tangents (e.g., tangent-radius perpendicularity, tangent-tangent segments), and chord relationships (e.g., perpendicular bisector, equal chords imply equal arcs).
  • Radians: defining and converting between radians and degrees, and understanding radian as a measure of angle.
  • Arc and sector calculations: computing arc length and sector area using both degree and radian measure.
  • Connections to triangles: identifying and using right-triangle structures within circles (e.g., inscribed right triangles, tangent-radius relationships) and applying similarity and trigonometry in circle contexts.
  • Proof and logic: constructing arguments and justifications using circle theorems, properties, and vocabulary.
  • Project-based skills: designing and presenting a Circle Design Challenge that synthesizes circle concepts, constructions, and geometric reasoning.

  • Tangent & chord theorem investigations (visual proofs; mid-module)
